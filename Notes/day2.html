1. Introduction to Text Pre-processing [00:01:51]

In Machine Learning and Deep Learning, algorithms fundamentally understand numbers (vectors and matrices), not raw text. Text Pre-processing is the systematic pipeline of converting unstructured text data into a clean, structured, and mathematical format.

Definition: Text Pre-processing

The sequence of cleaning and transforming raw text into a normalized format, ultimately mapping words to numerical vectors so that machine learning models can process them.

The Standard NLP Pipeline

Before we can classify sentiments or build chatbots, the text goes through several fundamental transformations.

Raw Text Data
➔
Cleaning (RegEx, Lowercasing)
➔
Tokenization
➔
Stemming / Lemmatization & Stopwords
➔
Vectorization (BoW, TF-IDF, Word2Vec)
Interview Tip: Why Pre-process?

Interviewers often ask why we can't just pass text directly to neural networks. The answer is twofold: Dimensionality (raw text has too many unique, unnormalized variations of the same word, causing sparse and massive input sizes) and Computability (models require numerical tensors to compute gradients).

2. Essential NLP Terminologies [00:07:08]

To communicate effectively as an NLP engineer, you must master the fundamental vocabulary. Let's break down the core hierarchy of textual data.

Term Definition Analogy / Example
Corpus The entire collection of text data you are working with. A complete book, or the combination of all customer reviews in your dataset.
Document A single, independent data point within the corpus. A single sentence, a single tweet, or one individual customer review (e.g., "The food is good").
Vocabulary The set of all unique words present across the entire corpus. A dictionary mapping every unique word in the dataset. If a dataset has 1 million words, the vocabulary might only be 10,000 unique words.
Word (Token) The smallest unit of text processed by the model. "food", "good", "pizza".
Example Breakdown

Corpus: [D1: "Pizza is good", D2: "Burger is bad"]

Documents: Two documents (D1 and D2).

Vocabulary: {"pizza", "is", "good", "burger", "bad"} (Count = 5 unique words).

3. One-Hot Encoding in NLP [00:17:34]

One-Hot Encoding (OHE) is the most primitive form of converting words into vectors. In this technique, each word in a document is represented by a vector of binary values.

How it Works

Unlike standard categorical OHE where the vector size equals the entire vocabulary, in basic NLP OHE, a sentence of N words is represented as a matrix where each word is a vector of length N, with a 1 at its index and 0 elsewhere.

Document: "a man eat"

[1, 0, 0] ➔ "a"
[0, 1, 0] ➔ "man"
[0, 0, 1] ➔ "eat"
Why One-Hot Encoding Fails for Text [00:23:00]
  • Sparsity: Matrices become massive and filled mostly with zeros. This wastes RAM and massively increases computational time.
  • Out of Vocabulary (OOV): If the test data contains a word not present in the training set (e.g., "dog"), the model instantly breaks because it has no predefined vector position for it.
  • Variable Lengths: Machine Learning models expect fixed-size inputs. OHE creates matrices based on sentence length (e.g., a 4-word sentence yields a 4x4 representation, a 3-word sentence yields a 3x3). Models cannot train on varying input shapes.
  • No Semantic Meaning: The distance between the vector for "king" and "queen" is exactly the same as the distance between "king" and "apple". It captures zero relational context.

4. Bag of Words (BoW) & Binary BoW [00:31:24]

To fix the variable length issue of One-Hot Encoding, we use the Bag of Words approach. BoW treats a document as an unordered "bag" (multiset) of words, ignoring grammar and word order but maintaining multiplicity (frequency).

Step-by-Step Mechanism

  1. Clean Text: Lowercase all words and remove punctuation.
  2. Remove Stop Words: Strip out non-informative words like "is", "a", "the", "and".
  3. Build Vocabulary: Extract all unique remaining words across the corpus. Order them usually by frequency.
  4. Vectorize: For each document, create a vector of the size of the vocabulary. Fill the vector with the frequency count of each word.
Real-World Example

D1: "He is a good boy"
D2: "She is a good girl"
D3: "Boy and girl are good"

After stop word removal (is, a, he, she, and, are):

D1: "good boy"
D2: "good girl"
D3: "boy girl good"

Vocabulary (by descending overall frequency): [Feature 1: "good", Feature 2: "boy", Feature 3: "girl"]

Document "good" (F1) "boy" (F2) "girl" (F3) Final Vector
D1 1 1 0 [1, 1, 0]
D2 1 0 1 [1, 0, 1]
D3 1 1 1 [1, 1, 1]

Binary Bag of Words [00:39:56]

In standard BoW, if the word "good" appears 5 times in a document, the vector holds the integer 5. In Binary BoW, we only care about presence vs. absence. If a word appears 1 or 100 times, the value is simply clamped to 1. This is excellent for simple Sentiment Analysis where the mere presence of a negative word dictates the sentiment, regardless of frequency.

Advantages vs Disadvantages of BoW [00:42:40]

Advantages: Simple, intuitive, fixes variable size problems (all vectors are now the size of the global vocabulary).

Disadvantages:

  • Sparsity remains: A 10,000-word vocabulary means every 5-word sentence is represented by 9,995 zeros.
  • Out of Vocabulary (OOV): New words in test data are simply ignored and dropped.
  • Word Ordering is Lost: "Good boy" and "Boy good" produce the exact same vector. Grammar is destroyed.
  • Semantic Meaning is Lost: Synonyms are treated as entirely different features.

5. Understanding Semantic Meaning & Distance [00:47:05]

When we convert text to vectors, we map them as points in high-dimensional space. How do we tell if two sentences are similar? We calculate the distance between their vectors.

In NLP (and heavily in Recommendation Systems), we avoid Euclidean distance due to the "curse of dimensionality". Instead, we use Cosine Similarity.

Cosine Similarity

Cosine similarity measures the cosine of the angle between two vectors projected in a multi-dimensional space. It evaluates the orientation rather than magnitude.

Formula Intuition: Similarity = $cos(\theta)$.

  • If angle is 0° (vectors overlap): $cos(0) = 1$ (100% Similar)
  • If angle is 90° (orthogonal): $cos(90) = 0$ (0% Similar)
Why BoW struggles with Semantics

Because BoW treats every word as a separate orthogonal axis, "Iron Man" and "Avengers" (while semantically related movies) might share no words in their plot summaries, resulting in orthogonal vectors and a cosine similarity of 0, missing the contextual relationship.

6. N-Grams (Unigrams, Bigrams, Trigrams) [00:53:53]

Bag of Words completely destroys word order. To retain local word ordering and capture multi-word contextual meaning, we use N-Grams.

What are N-Grams?

An N-gram is a contiguous sequence of n items (words) from a given sample of text. Instead of treating single words as features, we treat combinations of words as features.

Type Definition Example for "Krish eats food"
Unigram (1-gram) Single words (Standard BoW) "Krish", "eats", "food"
Bigram (2-gram) Combinations of 2 adjacent words "Krish eats", "eats food"
Trigram (3-gram) Combinations of 3 adjacent words "Krish eats food"
Interview Question: How to use multiple N-grams?

Q: Can we use unigrams, bigrams, and trigrams simultaneously in a model?

A: Yes! In Scikit-Learn's `CountVectorizer`, you can pass the parameter ngram_range=(1, 3). This instructs the vectorizer to generate features for unigrams, bigrams, AND trigrams combined. For example, "I am not feeling well" will generate features like: "I", "am", "I am", "am not", "I am not", etc.

Advantages of N-Grams: Retains context. "Not good" (Bigram) perfectly captures negative sentiment, whereas Unigrams ("Not", "good") might confuse the model into thinking it's positive.

7. Practical Python Implementation [01:02:10]

Below is the complete, production-ready pipeline for executing text preprocessing and generating a Bag of Words matrix using nltk and scikit-learn.

Python
# 1. Import necessary libraries
import nltk
import re
from nltk.corpus import stopwords
from nltk.stem import PorterStemmer, WordNetLemmatizer
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import CountVectorizer

# Download required NLTK data files (Run once)
# nltk.download('punkt')
# nltk.download('stopwords')
# nltk.download('wordnet')

# 2. Sample Data
paragraph = """Narendra Modi is the Prime Minister of India. 
He was the Chief Minister of Gujarat. 
People watch his YouTube channel!"""

# 3. Tokenize into sentences
sentences = nltk.sent_tokenize(paragraph)

# Initialize Stemmer and Lemmatizer
stemmer = PorterStemmer()
lemmatizer = WordNetLemmatizer()

# 4. Text Cleaning & Pre-processing Pipeline
corpus = []
for i in range(len(sentences)):
    # Remove special characters using Regex (Keep only a-z and A-Z)
    review = re.sub('[^a-zA-Z]', ' ', sentences[i])
    
    # Convert to lowercase
    review = review.lower()
    
    # Split into words
    review = review.split()
    
    # Remove stopwords and apply Lemmatization
    review = [lemmatizer.lemmatize(word) for word in review if word not in set(stopwords.words('english'))]
    
    # Rejoin the words back into a sentence
    review = ' '.join(review)
    corpus.append(review)

print(corpus)
# Output: ['narendra modi prime minister india', 'chief minister gujarat', 'people watch youtube channel']

# 5. Apply Bag of Words (CountVectorizer) [01:20:00]
# Initialize vectorizer (use binary=True for Binary BoW)
cv = CountVectorizer(binary=False, ngram_range=(1, 1)) 

# Transform corpus into feature matrix
X = cv.fit_transform(corpus).toarray()

# View Vocabulary and Matrix
print(cv.vocabulary_) 
print(X)
Important Scikit-Learn Hyperparameters
  • binary=True : Enforces Binary Bag of Words (0s and 1s only).
  • ngram_range=(1, 3) : Creates unigrams, bigrams, and trigrams.
  • max_features=1500 : Limits the vocabulary to the top 1500 most frequent words, drastically reducing sparsity and memory consumption.

8. Final Cheat Sheet & Revision Notes

Quick Recap

The Core Problem: NLP requires converting text to numbers. Simple encoding creates sparse, massive matrices with no context.

Step 1: Text Cleaning. Use Regex re.sub('[^a-zA-Z]', ' ', text) to drop punctuation. Convert to lowercase.

Step 2: Tokenization. Use nltk.sent_tokenize (sentences) and nltk.word_tokenize (words).

Step 3: Reduction. Use stopwords to remove fluff. Use Stemming (fast, cuts off suffixes, sometimes creates meaningless words like "histori") or Lemmatization (slower, dictionary-based, creates meaningful root words like "history").

Step 4: Vectorization.
• OHE: Terrible for text.
• Bag of Words (BoW): Counts word frequencies. Good for simple tasks, bad for grammar/semantics. Causes sparse matrices.
• N-Grams: Groups neighboring words to maintain local context (e.g., Bigrams, Trigrams).

Frequently Asked Interview Questions

Question Expected Answer
Stemming vs. Lemmatization? Stemming is a rule-based heuristic that hacks off suffixes (fast, prone to non-dictionary words). Lemmatization uses morphological analysis and a dictionary (WordNet) to return a valid base word (slower but highly accurate).
What is the Sparsity Problem? When vectorizing text, the vocabulary is huge (e.g., 10,000 words). A single sentence might have 5 words, making the 10,000-dimensional vector 99.9% zeros. This wastes memory and computation.
How do you solve Out of Vocabulary (OOV) in BoW? BoW inherently drops OOV words. To solve this properly, advanced techniques like Word2Vec, GloVe, or Subword Tokenization (used in BERT) are required.
When would you use Binary BoW over standard BoW? In tasks like Sentiment Analysis or Spam Detection, the mere presence of a highly polarized word (e.g., "terrible" or "lottery") is enough to classify the document, rendering exact frequency counts unnecessary.
