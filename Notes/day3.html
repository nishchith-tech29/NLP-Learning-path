[00:06:32] 1. Introduction & Revision of Bag of Words (BoW)

Before diving into advanced techniques, it is essential to revisit the foundational concept of converting text into vectors: Bag of Words (BoW). Machine learning models cannot understand raw text; they only comprehend numbers. BoW is the simplest technique to translate textual documents into a structured matrix of numbers.

📖 Definition: Bag of Words

A natural language processing technique that extracts features from text by representing the occurrence (frequency) of words within a document, completely ignoring the order and context of the words (hence, a "bag").

How Bag of Words Works (Pipeline)

To convert sentences to vectors, text must pass through a strict preprocessing pipeline:

Raw Text
→
Tokenization
→
Stop Words Removal & Lowercasing
→
Stemming / Lemmatization
→
Frequency Matrix (BoW)
💡 Example Pipeline

Input Sentences:

  • S1: "He is a good boy"
  • S2: "She is a good girl"
  • S3: "Boy and girl are good"

After Stop words (is, a, he, she, and, are) & Lowercasing:

  • S1: good boy
  • S2: good girl
  • S3: boy girl good

Vocabulary: [boy, girl, good]

Output Vectors:

  • S1: [1, 0, 1]
  • S2: [0, 1, 1]
  • S3: [1, 1, 1]

[00:11:37] 2. The Limitations of Bag of Words

While conceptually intuitive, the Bag of Words model suffers from critical architectural flaws when applied to real-world, large-scale NLP problems.

⚠️ The 3 Major Problems with BoW
  1. Sparsity Issue: If a corpus contains 10,000 unique words, each sentence is represented by a vector of 10,000 dimensions. Most of these values will be 0. This creates a vast sparse matrix that consumes immense memory and computational power.
  2. Out of Vocabulary (OOV) Issue: If the model is trained on a specific vocabulary, and a new sentence during testing contains an unseen word (e.g., "cat"), the BoW model will simply ignore it, losing valuable information.
  3. Loss of Semantic Meaning & Word Importance: BoW treats all words equally based solely on frequency. For example, if "The food is good" and "The food is not good" are processed, and "not" is mistakenly removed as a stop word, the vectors become identical. Furthermore, highly frequent words (like "good" across many reviews) dominate the matrix, even if they aren't uniquely identifying for a specific document.
🧠 Memory Trick

Think of Bag of Words like a popularity contest. The loudest (most frequent) words win, but they might not be the smartest or most informative words.

[00:14:55] 3. Introduction to TF-IDF

To fix the semantic meaning issue of BoW, we introduce TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency). The core philosophy of TF-IDF is to penalize highly frequent words that appear across all documents and reward rare words that appear in a specific document, as they carry the actual distinct meaning.

📖 Definition: TF-IDF

A statistical measure that evaluates how relevant a word is to a document in a collection of documents. This is done by multiplying two metrics: how many times a word appears in a document (TF), and the inverse document frequency of the word across a set of documents (IDF).

The Two Pillars of TF-IDF

Term Frequency (TF)
Captures the local importance of a word.
"How often does the word appear in THIS specific sentence?"
✖
Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)
Captures the global rarity of a word.
"How rare is this word across ALL sentences?"
🟰
TF-IDF Weight
True importance of the word.

Industry Use Cases: Document retrieval (Search Engines like Google), Keyword extraction, Text summarization, and spam detection pipelines.

[00:20:56] 4. The Mathematics: Step-by-Step Calculation

Let us demystify the mathematics behind TF-IDF using textbook formulas. A deep understanding of these formulas is required to clear advanced data science interviews.

🧮 Important Formulas

Term Frequency (TF):
TF = (Number of repetitions of word 'w' in sentence) / (Total number of words in sentence)

Inverse Document Frequency (IDF):
IDF = log_e (Total number of sentences / Number of sentences containing the word 'w')

Final TF-IDF Score = TF * IDF

Step-by-Step Practical Calculation [00:22:57]

Let's use our cleaned corpus:
S1: good boy
S2: good girl
S3: boy girl good
Total Sentences (N) = 3

Step 1: Calculate TF

Word S1 (2 words) S2 (2 words) S3 (3 words)
good 1/2 = 0.5 1/2 = 0.5 1/3 ≈ 0.33
boy 1/2 = 0.5 0/2 = 0 1/3 ≈ 0.33
girl 0/2 = 0 1/2 = 0.5 1/3 ≈ 0.33

Step 2: Calculate IDF [00:27:47]

Word Sentences Containing Word IDF Calculation Result
good 3 (S1, S2, S3) log_e (3/3) = log_e(1) 0
boy 2 (S1, S3) log_e (3/2) ~0.405
girl 2 (S2, S3) log_e (3/2) ~0.405

Step 3: Calculate Final TF-IDF Matrix (TF * IDF) [00:30:17]

Notice the magic here: Because "good" is present in every sentence, its IDF is 0. Therefore, its final TF-IDF score for every sentence becomes 0. The algorithm has successfully identified that "good" is a common, uninformative word!

Word S1 Vector S2 Vector S3 Vector
good 0.5 * 0 = 0 0.5 * 0 = 0 0.33 * 0 = 0
boy 0.5 * 0.405 = 0.202 0 * 0.405 = 0 0.33 * 0.405 = 0.133
girl 0 * 0.405 = 0 0.5 * 0.405 = 0.202 0.33 * 0.405 = 0.133
✅ Advantage Achieved [00:34:05]

Word importance is finally captured! Rare words that define the specific context of a document get higher weights, while universally common words are zeroed out or heavily penalized. This vastly improves model accuracy for classification tasks.

[00:35:33] 5. Practical Implementation using Python

In industry, we rely on the scikit-learn library to handle this math efficiently. Below is the production-grade implementation code.

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer
import pandas as pd

# 1. Define the cleaned corpus
corpus = [
    "good boy",
    "good girl",
    "boy girl good"
]

# 2. Initialize the Vectorizer
# (Scikit-learn implements a slightly smoothed version of the IDF formula by default to avoid zero division)
tfidf = TfidfVectorizer()

# 3. Fit and Transform the text data
X = tfidf.fit_transform(corpus)

# 4. View Results in a Pandas DataFrame for readability
feature_names = tfidf.get_feature_names_out()
df = pd.DataFrame(X.toarray(), columns=feature_names)

print(df)

[00:42:23] 6. N-Grams and Feature Optimization

When applying Bag of Words (CountVectorizer) or TfidfVectorizer, single words (unigrams) might not capture enough context. For example, "not good" has a completely different meaning than "not" and "good" separately. This is solved by N-Grams.

📖 Definition: N-Gram

A contiguous sequence of n items from a given sample of text.
• Unigram: "subscribe", "to", "krish"
• Bigram (2-gram): "subscribe to", "to krish"
• Trigram (3-gram): "subscribe to krish"

Using ngram_range in Scikit-Learn

By passing ngram_range=(2, 3), the vectorizer will generate both bigrams and trigrams, enriching the feature set with semantic phrases.

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import CountVectorizer

# Extracts Bigrams and Trigrams combined
cv = CountVectorizer(ngram_range=(2, 3)) 
X_ngrams = cv.fit_transform(corpus)

Managing Sparsity with max_features [00:48:40]

If you create trigrams for a large dataset, your feature space will explode, leading to the Curse of Dimensionality. To prevent this, use the max_features parameter.

Original Corpus: 50,000 unique words/phrases
↓
TfidfVectorizer(max_features=5000)
↓
Keeps only the top 5,000 most frequent terms. Drops the rest.

This single hyperparameter vastly reduces memory usage and speeds up model training times.

7. Interview Preparation & Questions

Be prepared to answer these highly requested interview questions regarding text vectorization techniques.

🎤 Q1: What is the primary difference between Bag of Words and TF-IDF?

Expected Answer: Bag of Words (BoW) creates vectors based purely on the raw frequency/count of words in a document. It treats all words equally. TF-IDF, however, scales down the frequency of words that occur universally across all documents (like 'is', 'the', 'good') and scales up rare, specific terms. Thus, TF-IDF captures the semantic importance of words better than BoW.

🎤 Q2: Does TF-IDF solve the Out of Vocabulary (OOV) problem?

Expected Answer: No. Neither Bag of Words nor TF-IDF solves the OOV problem. If a word is missing during the training phase (fitting the vocabulary), both algorithms will simply drop/ignore the word if it appears during testing. To solve the OOV and pure semantic relationships, we must shift to Word Embeddings like Word2Vec or GloVe.

🎤 Q3: Why is standardizing or limiting vocabulary important?

Expected Answer: Because NLP algorithms suffer from sparsity. If a corpus has 100,000 unique terms, every sentence is represented by a 100k-dimensional vector mostly filled with zeroes. This takes massive RAM and computing power. Limiting vocabulary using parameters like max_features restricts the model to the top N most frequent terms, reducing dimensionality while preserving the most crucial data.

Comparison Table: BoW vs TF-IDF

Feature Bag of Words (CountVectorizer) TF-IDF (TfidfVectorizer)
Values generated Integers (Counts: 0, 1, 2...) Floats (Ratios: 0.0 to 1.0)

8. Final Cheat Sheet & Roadmap

⚡ Quick Revision Cheat Sheet
  • Bag of Words (BoW): Text -> Word Frequencies. Suffers from sparsity and equal word weightage.
  • TF (Term Frequency): Occurrence of word in a document / Total words in the document.
  • IDF (Inverse Document Frequency): log(Total Documents / Documents containing the word).
  • TF-IDF Weight: TF × IDF. Penalizes common terms, highlights document-specific terms.
  • N-Grams: Combining adjacent words to preserve local sequence (e.g., bigrams, trigrams).
  • max_features: Sklearn parameter to control matrix sparsity by selecting top N highest-frequency words across the corpus.
  • Scikit-Learn Classes: CountVectorizer for BoW, TfidfVectorizer for TF-IDF.

Roadmap for Next Topics

While TF-IDF is a massive step up from Bag of Words, it still fundamentally fails to understand true semantic meaning (e.g., that "King" and "Queen" are related concepts, or dealing with unseen OOV words). To solve this, the NLP industry relies on Deep Learning techniques.
